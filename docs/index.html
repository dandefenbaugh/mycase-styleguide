Content

Thoughtful, consistent interface content is a core element of a well-designed user experience.

Encourage action

People use MyCase to get things done. Content should be written and structured to help users understand and take the most important actions.

  • Calls to action on buttons and links should start with a strong verb that describes the action the user will take when they click.
  • Always prioritize the most important information and task—don’t make users dig to find what they care about.
  • Break down complicated tasks into steps that help users focus on one thing at a time.
  • Use the active voice to clarify the subject and the action.

Be consistent

To help users understand key concepts and actions they can take, use consistent nouns (words used to identify a people, places, or things) and verbs (action words) wherever possible.

  • Get in the habit of making a list of all the most important verbs and nouns in the experience you’re building:
  • Look at your word list. Does each word clearly describe the object or action it represents in the simplest way possible?
  • Does your language reflect how users think and the words they use?
  • Identify synonyms (a word or phrase that means exactly or nearly the same as another word or phrase in the same language), and eliminate them. Each important object and action should have a single word to represent it.

Write for a grade 7 reading level

MyCase customers are busy people who may be running their firm in addition to managing their family life, and doing a million other things. They’re also located all over the country, have varying levels of literacy, and some may not speak English as their first language.

Writing for a grade 7 reading level doesn’t mean dumbing content down. It’s about making sure language is straightforward and communicates concepts as efficiently as possible.

  • Write short sentences (no more than 15–20 words).
  • Use headings and bullets to make your content easier to scan.
  • Avoid jargon and always choose a short, simple word over a long and complicated one.
  • Only use industry-standard terminology when you have reason to believe it will improve user understanding. Spend time researching what words people use, rather than defaulting to what corporations call things.
  • Edit unnecessary or repeated words.
  • Write for small screens first. Constraints can help you focus on the most important message.
  • Read your content out loud. If you get tripped up or it doesn’t sound like something a human would say, your content needs to be edited.

Voice & Tone

MyCase's voice is a reflection of who we are. We should always sound like MyCase.

Our tone adapts appropriately to the context and situation. The best way to decide what tone to use is to ask yourself what the user is likely feeling when they encounter your message so you can decide how to respond.

For example, when a user receives payment for an invoice for the first time, they might be feeling excited, accomplished, and maybe a little bit unsure of what to do next. We can respond to this by congratulating them and giving them the information they need to process their order.

When a user encounters an error message, they might be feeling anxious, stressed out, and worried about the impact it could have on their business. We can respond to this by stating what went wrong and giving clear direction about how to fix the problem. We never want to use a negative or frightening tone.

Confident, not arrogant

  • Use expertise to help users succeed, don’t talk down to them
  • Be plain-spoken, not pretentious
  • Be specific when explaining a user benefit without making a feature sound better than it is

Empathetic, not overprotective

  • Communicate in a way that respects user emotions and situations
  • Offer the user encouragement and practical advice
  • Clearly explain how the user will be impacted when something goes wrong, but don’t over-explain with redundant content

Transparent, not blunt

  • Be upfront and honest with users, even if we make a mistake
  • Back up claims with facts and data
  • Communicate with clarity and empathy—be direct, not insensitive

Dates & Times

Within the current day, display the time using uppercase AM or PM, without periods

2:00 PM

If you are using the 24-hour clock, display the time without AM/PM

14:00

Use a short month. Within the current calendar year, display the date without the year

Jan 14

Otherwise, display the date and year: Jan 14, 2019

Jan 14, 2019

Approximate time rounds down to the largest and most recent date or time unit

5 min ago, 3 days ago

When approximate time isn’t appropriate, display the specific date and/or time

Jan 14, 10:00  AM

Refer to yesterday or tomorrow in those terms

Today, 10:00 AM

When referring to a day of the week, such as for Calendar events, display the abbreviated day separated by a comma

Mon, Jan 10, 8:00 AM

If the day is in the upcoming or previ ous  week, display the day of the week

Mon, Jan 10

Separate a range of dates or times with an en dash

8 AM – 12:30 PM

Show the year on both the start and end...

Dec 6, 201 7  – Jan 2, 201 9

... unless both dates have the current year

Jan 4 – 6, 2019

Use a single AM or PM at the end of the range, if both times have the same AM/PM.

8 –  9 AM

Abbreviations

Use abbreviations for months, days of the week, hours and minutes

Mon, Mar 12, 2012

8 hrs ago

22 min ago

Abbreviated numerical units, which remove the “:00,” are appropriate for timestamps, durations, etc.

8 AM, 2 hrs ago

Describe a time of day in other ways that promote understanding

“Store open 9:00 AM–Midnight” or “Reminder for tomorrow afternoon”

Baseline ellipses [...] represent letters, words, or phrases that aren’t shown. They also represent when a name or email address is only partially visible.

"Subject: Writing to sa..."

To redact most numbers, like Social Security numbers, use three midline ellipses

321 • • • 4567

To redact credit and debit card data use four midline ellipses

Visa • • • • 1234

Additional Guidelines

  • For dollar amounts, use the dollar sign before the amount. Include a decimal and number of cents if more than 0: $20, $19.99
  • Use dashes without spaces between numbers in phone numbers: 619-234-5678
  • Don't use ampersands unless one is part of a company or brand name.
  • When referring generally to a file extension type, use all uppercase without a period. Add a lowercase s to make plural: JPG, PNGs
  • Only use “link” as an action. Do not use “[item] link” as a noun. For example, use “Case” or “Cases” instead of “Case Link” or “Case Links”. You can “link a case” but do not “add a case link”
  • Do not reiterate the item name in an item’s details. For example, use “Info” instead of “Contact Info” when on a contact details page.
  • In general, do not include “new” when adding items. Added items are “new” by default. For example, “Add Case”. (Occasionally “new” may be included in certain instances for clarity. For example, “Add new contact” when the option is displayed next to and as an alternative to “Add an existing contact”.)
  • Always use a serial comma (Oxford comma) when separating a list of three or more items.

Tables

  • Numerical data is right-aligned
  • Textual data is left-aligned
  • Headers are aligned with their data
  • Don’t use center alignment.
  • If all values in a column are the same, remove that column

Error Messages

There are four main types of messages: Errors, Confirmations, Warnings, and Notifications. If a message has different audiences, create separate text for each audience.

A well-written error message tells you, the user, the following:

  • What has happened
  • Why has it occurred
  • How it impacts you and
  • What you, the user, can do to prevent it happening again?

The error message should include enough information to solve the problem.

Guidelines

  • Use a positive tone: avoid condemnation.
  • Use consistent visual formats and placement.
  • Use consistent grammar and terminology your audience will understand.
  • Use Plain English – plain in the sense that it is concise, accurate, and avoid any unnecessary words.
  • Use user-centered phrasing
  • Be constructive. Indicate that something has gone wrong, but then help the user understand what to do next.
  • Be as specific and precise as possible.
  • Write error messages that educate and teach the user how to use the application. Users only read documentation as a last resort. Nice examples always help.
  • Use consistent grammar, terminology, and abbreviations.

Best Practices

  • Avoid the word “bad”. Use more descriptive terms to tell the user what is wrong.
  • Avoid the word “please”. It can be interpreted to mean that a required action is optional.
  • Avoid UPPERCASE text and exclamation points.
  • Explain the solution to the problem. If it has more than one step, link to a Help page that explains the task in detail.
  • Use active voice if possible.
  • Use passive voice to describe the error condition.

Buttons

Buttons make common actions immediately visible and easy to perform with one click or tap. Buttons communicate the action that will occur when the user touches them.

Primary Button

Use to highlight the most important actions in any experience. Primary buttons should only appear once per screen but not every screen needs a primary button.

Secondary Button

Used most in the interface. Only use another style if a button requires more or less visual weight.

Cancel Button

Use for "Cancel" in conjunction with primary and secondary buttons. Styled as a standard link.

Payments Button

Unique yellow background lets the user know these are payment-related actions.

Danger Button

Destructive buttons should trigger a confirmation dialog before the action is completed. Be thoughtful about using destructive buttons because they can feel stressful for users. Red background lets the user know these are destructive actions.

Secondary Danger Button

Used for destructive actions when not the primary action on the page

Small Buttons

Smaller versions of the primary and secondary buttons for use in placements where a regular button won’t fit.

Icon Buttons

Use when a toolbar or standard buttons would draw attention away from more important content. Use when space is constrained and the function of the button is obvious.

Guidelines

  • Button labels are sentence case and are as short as possible, while clearly explaining what happens when the button is pressed.
  • Buttons should be clearly and accurately labeled.
  • Labels should lead with strong, actionable verbs.
  • Use established button colors appropriately. For example, only use a red button for an action that’s difficult or impossible to undo.
  • Prioritize the most important actions. Too many calls to action can cause confusion and make users unsure of what to do next.
  • Position buttons in consistent locations in the interface.

Use dropdowns to let users select an action or option from a set of choices. It works well for lists of more than four choices when displaying them could clutter up the interface.

Examples

Guidelines

  • Typically, drop menus are used when you have 5-15 options. For anything with less options, consider radio buttons or checkboxes.
  • Sort the list in a logical order. For instance, put the most selected option at the top.
  • Simplify. Does an option rarely get selected? Re-evaluate if you really need that option.
  • Is it a yes or no selection? Try a toggles instead.
  • Need an option to turn something on or off? Use a checkbox.
  • Need to select a single option from a set of options that are all visible on the page? Use radio buttons.

Modals

Modals are overlays that prevent users from interacting with the rest of the application until a specific action is taken. Use modal dialogs when you need a response from a user, to reveal critical information, or to show info without losing the context of the page.

Standard Modal

A standard modal features a title and close button on top, with a primary action button and cancel link in the footer. Both the close button and cancel button close the modal.

Complex Modal

Modals can also feature multiple buttons and an additional note. For modals with more than one button, buttons are aligned to the right, and ordered with the primary action rightmost, then any secondary actions, and the cancel link last.

Modal with Sharing

Some modals include controls for sharing with other users or clients. These controls are attached to the right side of the modal and scroll separately from the content of the modal depending on their length.

Guidelines

Modals can be disruptive because they require an action be taken before they can continue interacting with the rest of MyCase. They should be used thoughtfully and sparingly.

Tables

Use tables to logically structure content in a grid to make it easier to read, scan, and compare.

Examples

Sorting, Pagination, Tool Bar, Multi-select Batch Actions, Inline Actions...

Guidelines

  • In most cases, tables are as wide as the page. However, if you have limited content, excessive table width makes reading and comprehension difficult. To fix this, reduce the table width to fit the content.
  • Organize columns and rows based on the information needs of your users. To help people read the table, order the columns by importance from left to right.
  • Left align non-numeric values and right align numeric values. The alignment of column headers should match the content alignment. Don’t center anything.
  • A borderless table can be useful when you want the information to feel more a part of the text it accompanies and extends.

Toggle

Toggles are a quick way to view and switch between enabled or disabled states.

The main difference between checkboxes, radio buttons, and a toggle is that the first two are for making a selection between options, and the the toggle is for turning an option on and off. If a physical switch would work for the action, a toggle is probably the best control to use.

Examples

Guidelines

  • Clearly indicate whether the setting is enabled or disabled and explain the implications of the state of the setting to users (e.g. “Automatic messages are disabled. Your user won’t receive automatic updates.”)
  • Clearly state when a setting or feature is not available and why. Provide actionable steps for users to unlock the functionality.

Tooltips

Tooltips are floating labels that briefly explain the function of a user interface element.

Use tooltips to identify or add a small amount of information to an element. Typically, tooltips are used to help users understand the meaning or purpose of icons, showing the full version of truncated text, or displaying the alt text for an image.

Examples

Guidelines

  • Provide useful, additional information or clarification.
  • Succinctly describe or expand on the element they point to.
  • Be provided for icon-only buttons.
  • Not be used to communicate critical information, including errors in forms or other interaction feedback.
  • Not contain any links or buttons.
  • Be used sparingly. If you’re building something that requires a lot of tooltips, work on clarifying the design and the language in the experience.
